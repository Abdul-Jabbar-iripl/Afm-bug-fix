Session 5.4

Conductivity for Jet Fuels

  • Jet fuel has conductivity in the range of 0 to 5 pS/m; that is, virtually no conductivity and is an insulator
  • The flow of fuel in the pipeline and various components across the supply chain generate static charges
  • Safe handling during distribution, refuelling and on-board the aircraft requires dissipation of static charges

    • 5.4.1 Conductivity Limits

  • The conductivity limits are mandatory for product to meet Def Stan 91-091 Standard
  • Limits for Electrical Conductivity for jet fuel is
    • Min 50 pS/m
    • Max 600 pS/m
  • When Static Dissipator Additive (SDA) is present, conductivity testing is required to ensure it meets the specification limits
  • Conductivity limits can be different for civil or military fuels
  • Civil fuels normally 50 – 600 pS/m
  • Military fuels can be 100 – 600 pS/m depending on the purchasing specification
    • ET+FSII:50 to 600 ps/m
    • JP-8 : 150 to 600 pS/m

    Low Conductivity

  • There are situations where conductivity can decrease rapidly and the fuel can fail to respond to additional dosing of SDA.
  • An industry investigation demonstrated that the static hazard was mitigated once conductivity was >20 pS/m

    • (JIG PQ Committee Report: Report on the Investigation into the Sydney Low Conductivity Incident, August 2005).

  • The current minimum 50 pS/m, therefore, represents a cautious doubling of the 20 pS/m minimum.
  • On this basis, and as an emergency provision when low conductivity occurs at airports, the local Competent Authority may decide to accept conductivities down to a minimum of 25 pS/m
  • The fuel to be supplied shall be fully tested according to the Standard and the Tank Release Note annotated with the explanation -

    • “Product released below 50 pS/m due to conductivity loss as per Annex F.2 in Defence Standard 91-091”.

    Effect of temperature on fuel electrical conductivity



    5.4.2 Static Dissipator Additive

  • The conductivity of the fuel is increased by the addition of static dissipator additive (SDA).
  • The addition of SDA speeds up the rate at which static charge can dissipate, thereby reducing the time for which a static hazard might exist.
  • SDA does not prevent the generation of static charges; therefore, bonding is essential when sampling, loading, or fueling aircraft.
  • Conductivity decreases as fuel moves through the supply chain.
  • Because of conductivity losses in the distribution system
    • Conductivity limits apply at the point of aircraft fueling at ambient temperature.
    • refinery may not be the best place to inject SDA, and it is more practical to inject SDA further downstream.
  • When SDA is not injected in a refinery, in such cases, the Certificate of Quality for the batch shall be annotated as follows: "Product meets requirements of Defense Standard 91-091 except for electrical conductivity
  • There is no requirement for a defined conductivity level when handling jet fuel on multi-product ships or pipelines.

    • 5.4.3 Testing Frequencies

  • Conductivity testing is required only if Static Dissipator Additive (SDA) is present in Jet Fuel
  • The conductivity of jet fuel containing SDA shall be checked at monthly intervals and recorded with the temperature of the fuel
  • It is encouraged and common practice of measuring conductivity at each tank release (which can be every few days at airports)

    • 5.4.4 Conductivity Meter

  • Apparatus
  • Digital Conductivity Meter, “EMCEE”
  • Model 1152-00-0000 manufactured by Emcee Electronics Inc., 177 Old Churchman Road, New Castle, Delaware 19720 U.S.A.


  • A portable battery-powered conductivity meter is used.
  • Its principle of operation involves applying a voltage across two (2) electrodes immersed in the fuel and the resulting current flow which is expressed as a conductivity value.
  • The electrodes consist of two (2) concentric stainless steel probes capable of measuring conductivity between zero (0) and 1999 Conductivity Units (C.U.).


  • If to be taken from within the tank:
  • “EMCEE” Extension Cable Kit Model 1152-06-000 available from the same source as previous.
  • A thermometer holder should be available so that the temperature can be determined.


  • Thermometer, having a suitable range for measuring fuel temperature in the field.
  • Sampling vessel, a clean stainless steel bucket of the type used for water drains, etc.
  • Approved CWD like Shell water detector kit


  • Each individual EMCEE meter has its own matched probe.
  • This probe has a calibration number stamped into the hexagonal section adjacent to the cylindrical portion containing holes.
  • It is important that, prior to use, the meter is checked to ensure that it is matched to the probe.
  • Incorrect measurements will occur if this is not done.


  • Follow the steps to check that the probe/meter calibration is correct:
    • Make sure the probe is clean as it may affect the test result
    • Cleaning process of the probe shall be according to the manufacturer’s recommendations
    • Attach the probe to the bottom of the meter.
    • While holding the meter with the probe OUT of the fuel, press the calibrate (marked “C”) button
  • The meter display should, after approximately three (3) seconds, be ten (10) times the number stamped on the probe ± 5.
  • For example: Probe Calibration Number = 40 Meter Reading (C) = 400 ± 5


  • If the reading is outside these limits, remove the probe and insert a small screwdriver into the three (3) mm diameter hole marked "CAL” on the right-hand side of the probe connector.

    • Note: Do not insert the screwdriver into the three (3)mm hole marked “ZERO” at this time.

  • While depressing the Calibrate (C) button, turn the screwdriver until the display is within the accepted limit.


  • To check that the ZERO adjustment is correct, depress the Measure (marked “M”) button with the probe OUT of the fuel.
  • Reading should be 000 ± 001 within approximately three (3) seconds.
  • If incorrect, remove the probe and proceed as follows:


  • Insert a small screwdriver into the 3 mm diameter hole marked ZERO to the left of the probe connector.


  • While depressing the Measure (M) button, turn the screwdriver until the display reads 000 ± 001.

    • Testing Procedures

  • Generally, it is preferable to measure the conductivity while the fuel is in the storage tank. Test which are taken from outside the tank may not be indicative of the conductivity of the product in the tank due to variations in temperature, sample may not be well represented of the entire depth of the product in the tank, improper cleaning of sampling containers, etc.
  • However, there are instances where conductivity testing of the fuel in the storage tank may be impractical (e.g. if the product level is too low for the probe on an extension cable to reach it). In this case, it will be necessary to draw a fuel sample.
  • In both cases, testing shall not be performed until the product has been allowed to settle. This requirement is essential as suspended water and turbulence will affect the reading obtained.


    • In-Storage Tank Test:

    1. Check the calibration of the conductivity meter.
    2. Touch the probe to bare metal on the tank structure (not at an open manway).
    3. Using the extension lead, lower the probe into the tank until it is approximately 150 mm (6 inches) below the surface of the fuel.

      4. Note: Avoid contact with tank wall, floating suctions, jiggle wires, etc.

    4. Move the conductivity probe in an up and down motion to remove previous fuel residue then hold it steady.
    5. Press the Measure (M) button ONLY and record the highest reading displayed after three (3) seconds).

      6. Note: Due to polarization of the fuel in the probe, the meter reading may continue to change. Only the reading three (3) seconds after depressing the Measure button is correct.

    6. Withdraw the probe and dry it. Rewind the extension lead.
    7. Lower the thermometer into the fuel to the same depth as the conductivity probe and allow it to stabilize for approximately two (2) minutes.
    8. Withdraw the thermometer as quickly as possible and record the reading.

    Running Sample Test:

    If possible, the sample should be drawn from multi-level tank sample points; otherwise, a running line sample should be drawn from the fast-flush system.





    1. The bucket should be cleaned thoroughly with Shell Chemical Solvent X55 or equivalent and then air dried.
    2. The lines to the sample points should be flushed of their volume.
    3. The bucket should be rinsed three (3) times with the product from the point to be sampled.
    4. Draw a sample of at least four (4) litres.
    5. After turbulence in the bucket has ceased, check for the complete absence of water (using a Shell Detector capsule); if water is detected, take more samples until a dry sample is obtained.
    6. Measure the conductivity and temperature with minimum delay
