Session 2.2

GENERAL FUEL SPECIFICATIONS

General

    2.2.1
  • This chapter aims to give a brief overview of the various properties of Jet A-1. Jet A-1 in India conforms to IS-1571, equivalent to DEFSTAN 91-91. The properties of Jet A-1 can be broken up into various groups as follows:
  • COMPOSITION: Appearance, Acidity, Aromatics, Olefins, and Sulphur
  • VOLATILITY: Distillation, Flash Point, or Density
  • FLUIDITY: Freezing Point/Viscosity
  • COMBUSTION: Specific Energy, Smoke Point, and Naphthalenes
  • CORROSION: Copper Strip Corrosion / Silver Strip Corrosion
  • STABILITY: Thermal Stability
  • CONTAMINANTS: Existent Gum / Particulates / Water Reaction /Water Separability
  • OTHERS: Electrical Conductivity / Lubricity
  • The test methods are as prescribed by ASTM (numbers prefixed with D) or IP (numbers prefixed with IP). s s
2.2.2 APPEARANCE
  • Significance: This indicates the presence of particulate matter or water in the fuel. The dirt would clog the fuel filters of the aircraft, whereas the water would freeze at the very low temperatures experienced at high altitudes and again choke the aircraft filters.
  • Test Method: D 4176.
  • Acceptable Limits: The sample should be bright, clear, and free of particulate matter and water.
  • Description of the Test: The sample is taken in a one-litre bottle and twirled to create a vortex. Free water and particulate matter would settle at the bottom of the vortex, whereas the hazy appearance of the sample would indicate suspended water.

    2.2.3 Acidity
  • Significance: The total acidity of combined organic and inorganic acids indicates the corrosive potential of fuel metals. A trace of organic acids can affect water separation properties.
  • Test Method: D 3242: Acidity in Aviation Turbine Fuel
  • Acceptable Limits: Max 0.015 mg KOH/g
  • Description of Test: A weighed sample is dissolved in titration solvent and titrated with alcoholic potassium hydroxide to a colorimetric endpoint. The amount of acidity in the fuel is expressed as mg KOH/g.

    2.2.4 AROMATICS
  • Significance: Aromatic content relates directly to flame radiation, carbon deposition, and smoke. It also affects the swelling of elastomers in the fuel system. Hydrogen content contributes to combustion cleanliness and is broadly related to aromatic content.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 1319: Hydrocarbon Types in Liquid Petroleum Products by Fluorescent Indicator Adsorption
  • Acceptable Limits: Max. 25% (by volume)
  • Description of the Test: A small amount of sample is placed at the top of a special glass adsorption column packed with activated silica gel. Prior to this, the top layer of gel had been treated with fluorescent dyes. Isopropyl alcohol is used to transport the sample and the fluorescent dyes down the column. The hydrocarbons separate into bands of aromatics, olefins, and saturates because of their different affinities for the gel. The fluorescent dyes, which also selectively separate, make the boundaries of the aromatic, olefin, and saturate zones visible under ultraviolet light.

    2.2.5 OLEFINS
  • Significance: Olefins are unsaturated hydrocarbons, which are potential contributors to instability in storage.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 1319 – Hydrocarbon Types in Liquid Petroleum Products by Fluorescent Indicator Adsorption.
  • Acceptable Limits: Max 25% (By volume)
  • Description of the Test: A small amount of sample is placed at the top of a special glass adsorption column packed with activated silica gel. Prior to this, the top layer of gel had been treated with fluorescent dyes. Isopropyl alcohol is used to transport the sample and the fluorescent dyes down the column. The hydrocarbons separate into bands of aromatics, olefins, and saturates because of their different affinities for the gel. The fluorescent dyes, which also selectively separate, make the boundaries of the aromatic, olefin, and saturate zones visible under ultraviolet light.

    2.2.6 SULPHUR
  • Significance: The presence of sulphur leads to the formation of sulphur oxides during combustion and can cause corrosion of turbine metal parts.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 1266 – Sulfur in Petroleum Products (Lamp Method)
  • Acceptable Limits: Max 0.30% (by weight)
  • Description of the Test: A sample is burned in a glass lamp with a cotton wick to oxidise the sulphur to sulphur dioxide. The combustion gases are bubbled through a hydrogen peroxide solution to convert the sulphur dioxide to sulphuric acid. The amount of sulfuric acid formed is measured either by barium precipitation or titration.

    2.2.7 MERCAPTAN SULPHUR
  • Significance: Mercaptan sulphur compounds have a very unpleasant odour and attack certain elastomer materials. This can affect the synthetic / rubber components in the aircraft fuel system.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 4952, Doctor Test
  • Acceptable Limits: Negative Result
  • Description of the Test: The sample is shaken with sodium plumbite solution, a small quantity of powdered sulphur is added, and the mixture is shaken again. The presence of mercaptans of hydrogen sulphide or both is indicated by the discoloration of the sulphur floating at the oil-water interface or by discolouration of either of the phases.

    2.2.8 DISTILLATION
  • Significance: The distillation curve defines the kerosene boiling range, which needs to be appropriate for balanced vaporisation of the whole fuel volume.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 86: Distillation of Petroleum Products
  • Acceptable Limits: The various values for the distillation are: 10% recovery at 205 oC (max), final BP at 300 oC (max), distillation residue of 1.5% (by volume max), and distillation loss of 1.5% (by volume max). Temperatures for 50% recovery and 90% recovery by volume are also to be reported.
  • Description of Test: A 100-ml sample is placed in a round-bottom flask and heated at a rate specified for the sample's vapour pressure characteristics. Vapour temperatures are recorded when the first drop of condensate is collected (initial boiling point) and at recovered volumes of 5 ml, 10 ml, 15 ml, 20 ml, and every subsequent 10-ml interval to 80 ml, 85 ml, 90 ml, 95 ml, and at the end of the test (end point). The amount of sample remaining in the flask at the end of the test and the amount lost during the test—both in percent by volume—are, respectively, recorded and calculated.

    2.2.9 FLASH POINT

  • Significance: A high flash point aids fire safety in fuel handling. Flash point is related to volatility and therefore affects combustibility.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 56: Flash Point by Tag Closed Tester
  • Acceptable Limits: Minimum 38 °C
  • Description of the Test: A sample is placed in a lidded cup and heated at a slow, constant rate. At regular intervals, the lid is opened, and an ignition source is directed into the cup. The lowest temperature at which the ignition source causes the vapour above the sample to ignite is the flash point.

    2.2.10 DENSITY

  • Significance: Density is required for aircraft weight loading calculations since fuel is delivered by volume. Density also relates to specific energy (energy per unit weight of the fuel).
  • Test Method: ASTM D 1298: Density, Relative Density (Specific Gravity), or API Gravity of Crude Petroleum and Liquid Petroleum Products by Hydrometer Method
  • Test Method: ASTM D 1298: Density, Relative Density (Specific Gravity), or API Gravity of Crude Petroleum and Liquid Petroleum Products by Hydrometer Method
  • Description of the Test: Fuel is transferred to a cylindrical container, and a hydrometer is carefully lowered into the cylinder and allowed to settle. After the temperature of the sample has equilibrated, the value on the hydrometer scale positioned at the surface of the sample and the sample temperature are recorded. The hydrometer value is converted to density at 15°C using standard tables.

    2.2.11 FREEZING POINT

  • Significance: As the fuel is cooled, the hydrocarbons solidify, forming wax crystals. The freezing point is an indicator of a fuel’s low-temperature pumpability.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 2386: Freezing Point of Aviation Fuels
  • Acceptable Limits: -47oC max.
  • Description of the Test: A sample is placed in a special jacketed tube fitted with a stirring rod and a thermometer. The tube is placed in a low-temperature bath and stirred as the sample cools. When crystals of hydrocarbon appear, the tube is removed from the cooling bath and allowed to warm up slowly with continuous stirring. The temperature at which the hydrocarbon crystals completely disappear is the freezing point.

    2.2.12 VISCOSITY

  • Significance: Viscosity is a measure of a liquid’s resistance to flow under pressure. Viscosity affects fuel pumpability over the operating temperature range and relates to droplet size in sprays produced in the burner nozzles. If viscosity is too high, an engine can be difficult to relight in flight.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 445: Kinematic Viscosity of Transparent and Opaque Liquids
  • Acceptable Limits: 8 centi-stoke (mm2/sec) max @ -20 oC
  • Description of the Test: A sample is placed in a calibrated glass viscometer and held at a closely controlled temperature. The time required for a specific volume of the sample to flow through the capillary under gravity is measured. This time is proportional to the kinematic viscosity of the sample.

    2.2.13 SPECIFIC ENERGY

  • Significance: Specific energy represents the amount of heat energy obtainable from a fuel to provide power. Fuels with higher specific energy are better for the aircraft.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 4809: Heat of Combustion of Liquid Hydrocarbon Fuels by Bomb Calorimeter Referee Method
  • Acceptable Limits: 42.8 MJ/kg (minimum).
  • Description of the Test: A weighed sample of fuel is placed in an oxygen bomb calorimeter under specified conditions. The fuel is ignited, and the temperature increase of the calorimeter is used to calculate the heat of combustion.

    2.2.14 SMOKE POINT

  • Significance: The smoke point indicates the tendency of a fuel to form soot, which is related to the type of hydrocarbons making up its composition.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 1322: Smoke Point of Kerosene and Aviation Turbine Fuel
  • Acceptable Limits: 18 mm (minimum).
  • Description of the Test: The sample is burned in a wick-fed lamp. The smoke point is the maximum height of flame that can be achieved without smoking.

    2.2.15 NAPHTHALENES

  • Significance: Naphthalenes are high in carbon content, exacerbating the problems of carbon formation, flame radiation, and smoke
  • Test Method: ASTM D 1840: Naphthalene Hydrocarbons in Aviation Turbine Fuels by Ultraviolet Spectrophotometry
  • Acceptable Limits: 1% by volume (maximum).
  • Description of the Test: A sample is dissolved in isooctane at a known concentration, and the absorbance of the solution at 285 nanometers is measured and used to calculate the naphthalene content.

    2.2.16 COPPER STRIP CORROSION

  • Significance: The copper strip corrosion test checks for the corrosive properties of jet fuel to ensure that the organic sulphur compounds will not corrode metallic components in the fuel system.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 130: Detection of Copper Corrosion from Petroleum Products by the Copper Strip Tarnish Test
  • Acceptable Limits: 1 (maximum) for 2 hours at 100 oC.
  • Description of Test: A polished copper strip is immersed in a sample for two hours at 100°C (212°F) and then removed and washed. The condition of the copper surface is qualitatively evaluated by comparing it to standards.

    2.2.17 SILVER STRIP CORROSION

  • Significance: The Silver Strip Corrosion Test checks for the corrosive properties of jet fuel to ensure that it will not corrode metallic components in the fuel system.
  • Test Method: IP 227
  • Acceptable Limits: 1 (maximum) for 4 hours at 50 oC.
  • Description of Test: A polished silver strip is immersed in a sample for two hours at 50°C and then removed and washed. The condition of the copper surface is qualitatively evaluated by comparing it to standards.

    2.2.18 THERMAL STABILITY

  • Significance: Thermal stability is one of the most critical jet fuel properties because the fuel serves as a heat exchange medium in the engine and airframe. Jet fuel removes heat from engine oil, hydraulic fluid, and air conditioning equipment. The heating of the fuel accelerates the reactions that lead to gum and particulate formation. Thermal stability relates to the amount of deposits formed in the engine fuel system at high temperatures.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 3241: Thermal Oxidation Stability of Aviation Turbine Fuels (JFTOT Procedure)
  • Test Method: ASTM D 3241: Thermal Oxidation Stability of Aviation Turbine Fuels (JFTOT Procedure)
  • Description of the Test: Fuel is pumped over a heated aluminium alloy tube at a constant flow rate for 2.5 hours. After contacting the tube, the fuel is filtered to collect any solid decomposition products. The pressure drop across the filter is monitored during the test. At the end of the test, the tube is removed and visually examined for any stain or discolouration, which is rated by comparing it to a standard colour scale. This visual tube rating and the pressure drop across the filter at the end of the test are reported as the test result.

    2.2.19 EXISTENT GUM

  • Significance: Existent gum is a non-volatile residue left after the evaporation of fuel. It also serves as a check for fuel contamination within the product distribution system.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 381: Gum Content in Fuels by Jet Evaporation
  • Acceptable Limits: 7 mg (maximum) per 100 ml.
  • Description of the Test: A measured quantity of fuel is transferred to a weighed beaker, placed in a heated bath, and evaporated under a flow of steam. The resulting residue is weighed and reported as existing gum

    2.2.20 WATER REACTION

  • Significance: Water reaction determines the presence of materials that react with water and affect the stability of the fuel-water interface.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 1094, Water Reaction of Aviation Fuels
  • Acceptable Limits: 1 b (maximum).
  • Description of the Test: A sample is shaken with an aqueous buffer solution in a clean glass cylinder at ambient temperature and then allowed to stand to let the mixture separate into hydrocarbon and aqueous phases. After five minutes of standing, the change in volume of the aqueous layer is measured. The appearance of the hydrocarbon/aqueous interface is also examined for the presence of bubbles or film.

    2.2.21 WATER SEPARABILITY

  • Significance: The Water Separation Index rates the ability of the fuel to release entrained or emulsified water when passed through a fibreglass filter coalescer and affects the settling of water in jet fuel.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 3948: Determining Water Separation Characteristics of Aviation Turbine Fuels by Portable Separometer Using a semiautomatic micro-separometer instrument
  • Acceptable Limits: 70 (minimum).
  • Description of the Test: A fuel sample is mixed with water, forced through a fibreglass coalescing medium, and rated. The MSEP rating indicates the relative ease of coalescing water from the sample. The instrument is calibrated with a water-free fuel sample.

    2.2.22 ELECTRICAL CONDUCTIVITY

  • Significance: The electrical conductivity of the jet needs to be high enough to dissipate any electrostatic charges generated during fuel handling operations to prevent fire or explosion hazards.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 2624: Electrical Conductivity of Aviation and Distillate Fuels
  • Acceptable Limits: 50–450 picosiemens per metre (or conductivity unit)
  • Description of the Test: A sample is placed in a cell containing two electrodes. A voltage is applied across the electrodes, and the resulting current is expressed in terms of the conductivity of the sample.

    2.2.23 LUBRICITY.

  • Significance: Jet fuel must possess a certain degree of lubricity because jet engines rely on the fuel to lubricate some moving parts in fuel pumps and flow control units.
  • Test Method: ASTM D 5001: Measurement of Lubricity of Aviation Turbine Fuels by the Ball-on-Cylinder Lubricity Evaluator (BOCLE)
  • Acceptable Limits: 0.85 mm (maximum) for civil aviation and 0.65 mm (maximum) for defence use.
  • Description of the Test: A non-rotating steel ball is held against a cylindrical ring. The cylindrical ring is rotated at a fixed speed while partially immersed in the fuel. At the end of the test, the ball is removed and examined for wear. The size of the wear scar is related to the lubricity of the fuel (a large wear scar means poor lubricity).
