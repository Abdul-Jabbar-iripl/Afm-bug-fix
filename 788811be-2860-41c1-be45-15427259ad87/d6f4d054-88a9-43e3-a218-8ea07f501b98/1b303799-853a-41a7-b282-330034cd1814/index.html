Session 2.1

General Fuel Grades

Fuel Grades

    2.1.1 Jet A-1
  • Aviation fuels have evolved over a period of time based on aircraft engine requirements. The initial piston-type engines used gasoline-based fuels. However, during World War II, due to the easy availability of kerosene (diesel and gasoline were in short supply as they were used for the war), turbine engines using kerosene were developed.
  • Over a period of time, gasoline type, kerosene type and even a mix of the two (known as wide-cut fuel as they spanned the gasoline and kerosene boiling ranges) were used to drive the aircraft engines. This chapter briefly explains the various fuels used to run aircraft engines.
  • Based on the type of fuel, trade names were developed. Fuels used in jet engines were called jet fuels (Jet A, Jet A-1, and Jet B) or Jet Propulsion (JP 5, and JP 8), whereas piston engines used gasoline, which was called aviation gasoline, or AVGAS.
    2.1.2 Jet A
  • Jet A is a kerosene fuel used extensively in the USA. It meets USA Specification ASTM D 1655. The primary difference between Jet A and Jet A-1 is the freeze point. Jet A has a higher freeze point of -40 C or below, while Jet A-1 must have a freeze point of -47 C or below. As a result, Jet A-1 is more suitable for operation at low temperatures, like the polar routes during the winter. Jet A is, however, extensively used in the US due to its comparatively lower costs and greater availability.

    2.1.3 Jet B
  • Jet B is a wide-cut fuel with excellent freezing properties, thus suitable for low-temperature operations. Jet B is currently only manufactured in Canada and used in Canada, including Alaska. Jet B meets Canadian Standard CAN/CGSB 3.22.

    2.1.4 JP-5
  • JP 5 is a high-flash-point kerosene fuel developed by the Air Force and Navy for use on aircraft carriers. Its flash point is a minimum of 60 oC, which provides for safer operations. JP 5 is also used by the Indian Navy for its aircraft and is supplied only by Indian Oil.

    2.1.5 JP-8
  • JP 8 is a military version of Jet A-1 with similar properties; however, the difference is in the additives used in JP 8. JP-8 has now been adopted as the standard US military fuel for all aircraft and vehicles.

    2.1.6 AVGAS
  • Avgas is a gasoline-based fuel used in piston-type engines. It was initially developed for military use and subsequently spread to commercial applications. Today, Avgas is used mainly by small aeroplanes and light helicopters. Still, many military and civilian transports are also powered by large piston engines that use Avgas. Two main specifications cover Avgas: ASTM D 910 and DEF STAN 91-90. Grades of Avgas are identified by their nominal minimum lean-mixture antiknock rating(s). Grade 100LL (100 Octane Low Lead) is the most popular grade of Avgas, whereas Grade 100 (100 Octane) and Grade 80 (80 Octane) are used sparingly.
