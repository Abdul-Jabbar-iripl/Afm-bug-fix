    7. MICROBIAL GROWTH ISSUES
    • As microbes are naturally present in storage and distribution systems, microbial growth in aviation fuel systems can lead to rapidly accelerated corrosion, filter blocking, and increased levels of tank contamination, requiring more frequent cleaning.
    • Monitoring for changes in microbial growth can provide early warning of potential problems and help prevent expensive and disruptive remediation.
    • This chapter provides a guide on microbial growth, how it occurs, and how it is avoided, including using IATA-recommended on-site microbiological test kits and interpretation of test results.
    8. FIELD TESTS
    • The number of tests performed on jet fuel decreases as the product moves closer to the airport. This is because as the product gets close to the airport, it is transported by a dedicated system.
    • Field tests are performed at the terminals, airport depot and into-plane to ensure that the fuel's physical properties have not changed as the jet fuel is transported across the supply chain. Field tests are essential for aviation fuel quality assurance and provide an early warning of product contamination.
    • This chapter will guide your understanding of what field tests are and what their significance is.

    15. NOZZLES AND COUPLERS
    • Refuelling nozzles are designed to simplify and maximise safety during refuelling operations.
    • Pressure Control Couplers are designed to provide a safe and accurate connection during aircraft refuelling. It connects to a hydrant pit valve or pit adapter to allow pressurised fuel to flow from an underground pipeline into the receiving aircraft. It incorporates a deadman-operated, direct-acting pressure regulator that protects the receiving aircraft from excess pressure and damage due to pressure surges.
    • This chapter provides guidance on the operations and maintenance of refuelling nozzles and couplers used in aviation into-plane operations, including hose end strainers, overwing nozzles and nozzle holders, testing frequencies, records, and forms.

    21. PRESSURE CONTROL
    • Aircraft fueling systems are designed for flow not exceeding the maximum pressure of 50 psi (3.5 bar) in the vehicle delivery nozzle and to withstand a maximum shock or surge in the range of 100 psi to 120 psi (6.9 to 8.3 bar).
    • To protect aircraft from excessive flow and surge (shock pressures), which can damage aircraft fuel systems, refuelling equipment delivering fuel into aircraft is fitted with pressure control systems.
    • This chapter describes the importance of pressure control systems, the principles of operation, pressure control testing, test requirements, and records.
